Bilingualism

Models of Bilingual Education

A bilingual education is a form of heteroglossia where two cultures are forced to be engaged through two different languages in the classroom. In the typical United States bilingual classroom, deemed by Sonia Nieto in Affirming Diversity as the “transitional bilingual education,” the main goal is to teach students english as a second language. In this model, the student receives a timeframe in which she is supposed to be fully fluent in English without any need for her native language. Although this is a popular and viable model, a more applicable bilingual education model to the topic of linguistic diversity is referred to by Nieto as the “maintenance bilingual education,” where the goal is not to transition the student from a native language to English, but rather to preserve both, giving the student the asset of a second language as well as preserving the culture that comes with it.

In addition, another model that aligns with the principles of applying heteroglossia is the “two-way bilingual education,” in which students of the minority language integrate with students of the majority language. In this model, our culturally sustaining pedagogy is achieved, as students from all backgrounds interact and acquire a mutual respect and understanding of all cultures.
